Pedal Visualizer

Helping students develop musical judgment, particularly in their use of the sustain pedal, remains one of the most elusive challenges in piano education.

Much of piano pedagogy for beginners focuses on concrete musical elements like pitch and rhythm, which students can relatively straightforwardly identify as "correct" or "incorrect" in practice. However, the technique of expert pianists demonstrates not just "correct" notes and rhythms, but more importantly an ability to obtain a greater variety of sounds and tone quality from the instrument.

Dynamics and articulation are two domains in which students can explore a greater variety of sound-types in their playing; for example, staccato (separated sounds), legato (connected or "linked" sounds), or marcato or sforzando accents (sharp or stressed sounds) involve different motions of the fingers, hands, and arms, and create distinct effects in the sound of the piano. Both dynamics and articulation are often somewhat difficult for students to master because they involve qualitative contrasts in playing, rather than quantitative certainties like pitch and rhythm.

One of the most difficult elements of tone for students to grasp is the use of the damper pedal, which removes the dampers from the piano's strings and allows played strings to resonate freely. The pedal allows the pianist to add varying degrees of resonance and depth to the instrument's tone, and to enhance simple dynamics and articulations by lifting and replacing the dampers.

To help students clear this hurdle faster, a novel approach might be suggested: one that uses a visual approach, instead of an auditory approach. Much research has been done on the difference between auditory and visual learning, with the end result being that visual learning is a dominant preference. For example, Jawed et al. found that nearly 65% of the population are visual learners. While this approach is obviously for more academic areas and not for music (a field that is defined by the auditory sense), I hypothesize that many piano students can benefit from a simple visualization of the audio emitted from the instrument.
